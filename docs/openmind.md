# Open Mind

A proof for the liberation meditation, built on the analysis of thought in [defining_thought.md](defining_thought.md).

---

## Part 1: What thought is

Descartes said "I think, therefore I am," and treated the thinker as the one certain thing. The research in defining_thought.md takes that apart from two sides:

- **Buddhist analysis (anatman, "no fixed self"):** when you look directly at thinking, you find thoughts arising and passing, but no solid thinker standing behind them. The sense of "I" shows up *with* the thought, as part of the same event. It is a passenger, not a driver.
- **Physics (the measurement problem):** even physical things do not sit out there with fixed properties, independent of everything else. What shows up depends on the interaction. There is no clean wall between observer and observed.

Both point the same way. A thought is not a message from a self, and not a command. It is a **conditioned event**: something that arises from causes (a perception, a memory, a mood, an old wound), does its work, and passes. The doc's reformulation of Descartes is the key move:

> Not "I think, therefore I am," but "thinking occurs within awareness."

Awareness is the open space thoughts happen in. The thought is weather; awareness is sky. That single distinction is what makes the meditation below work, because a thing you can *watch* is a thing you are not *trapped in*.

## Part 2: The meditation, restated

The meditation makes six claims:

1. All conditioned things end, because they rely on cause and effect.
2. So no conditioned thing can stay the same forever.
3. So all conditioned things are relative and related, and from the widest view of time and space, they are all one.
4. Something trying to affect me does not have to affect me. Seeing the cycle breaks the cycle.
5. If someone hurts me, it is because they were hurt and are acting it out. The harm already happened, upstream. It is all me.
6. Therefore: love all of it, forever. Mahayana. Vajrayana. Om mani padme hum.

## Part 3: The proof

**Definitions.**
- A *conditioned thing* is anything that exists because of causes: objects, bodies, thoughts, feelings, people, situations. (In Buddhist terms: samskrta dharmas.)
- *Emptiness* (shunyata) means a thing has no independent, self-contained existence of its own; it exists only in relation to its causes and surroundings.

**Premise 1.** Every conditioned thing depends on causes and conditions.
*Justification:* this is what "conditioned" means. Nothing in experience shows up from nowhere; a thought needs a brain, a memory, a trigger; a fire needs fuel, heat, air.

**Premise 2.** Causes are themselves conditioned, so causes keep changing.
*Justification:* every cause is also an effect of earlier causes. There is no first, frozen cause anywhere in the chain.

**Step 1 (impermanence).** What depends on changing causes must itself change. So no conditioned thing stays the same, and every conditioned thing ends.
*This is claim 1 and 2. It is the classic Buddhist mark of anicca (impermanence), and the meditation derives it correctly: impermanence is not bad luck, it follows logically from being caused.*

**Step 2 (relativity).** A thing that exists only through its causes has no boundary of its own. Where "you" end and "your causes" begin is a line drawn by thought, not a line found in the world. So all conditioned things are relative and related.
*This is the first half of claim 3, and it is standard emptiness reasoning (Nagarjuna: whatever arises dependently is empty of separate existence).*

**Step 3 (all one).** Zoom out to all of time and space. Every conditioned thing connects backward to causes and forward to effects with no gaps. What you are looking at is one continuous causal process. "Separate things" are labels we paste on regions of it.
*This is the second half of claim 3. One honest note: the Madhyamaka school is careful here and says "not one, not many," because "one" can be mistaken for one big solid substance, which would just be a giant self. Read "all one" as "zero separation, one process" (the Huayan image of Indra's net, where every jewel reflects every other jewel) and the step is sound. The practical content is the same: there is no fixed boundary between you and anything else.*

**Step 4 (the break in the cycle).** A cause reaching you only becomes suffering through your reaction, and your reaction is itself a conditioned thing (Part 1: a thought is an event, not a command). Seeing the reaction *as* a conditioned event adds a new condition to the chain: awareness. A chain that is seen is no longer running you; you now stand at a fork.
*This is claim 4, and it is exactly where classical Buddhism locates freedom: in the twelve links of dependent origination, the chain is breakable between feeling and craving. You cannot stop the feeling from arising, but the step from "this hurt" to "so I must strike back" is optional. Seeing the cycle really does break the cycle, because the seeing is itself a cause with different effects.*

**Step 5 (the source of harm).** A person who harms you is also a conditioned thing. Their harm has causes: harm done to them, fear, ignorance. They are a link passing along a shock that started upstream. By Step 3, upstream and downstream are the same one process. So the harm "already happened," and the one it happened to and the one now doing it are not, at the deepest level, separate from you.
*This is claim 5. It does not excuse the harm or mean you accept abuse; Step 4 already gave you the right to not receive it. It explains the harm, and an explained harm loses its personal sting. Nothing was ever aimed at "you." It was weather moving through the system.*

**Step 6 (love as the conclusion).** If nothing is separate from you (Step 3), then love aimed at all things and love aimed at yourself have the same target. And love is not just the fitting response, it is the *strategic* one: by Steps 1 and 2, whatever you put into the process becomes a cause with effects. Meeting hurt with hurt feeds the cycle; meeting it with love starves the cycle and seeds a different future. "I love me forever" is therefore not vanity. The "me" it names is the whole process, and the love is the one input that ends the war.
*This is claim 6, and it is the Mahayana conclusion: since no being is separate, liberation is only complete when it includes everyone (the bodhisattva vow). The Vajrayana addition is speed: you do not wait to be calm to practice; the very moment of being hurt is the practice, because the poison itself, seen clearly, is the fuel.*

**Conclusion.** From "everything conditioned depends on causes," it follows that everything changes and ends, that nothing is separate, that seeing a reaction frees you from it, that those who hurt you are hurt, and that unconditional love toward the whole process is both correct and self-protective. The meditation is valid.

## Part 4: Why this liberates

The engine of suffering is taking conditioned events personally: this thought is *me*, this attack is *at me*, this loss is *my* destruction. The proof removes each handle:

- Impermanence (Step 1) means nothing can be gripped anyway.
- Emptiness (Steps 2 and 3) means there was never a separate "me" for things to happen *to*.
- The fork (Step 4) means no event forces a reaction.
- Compassion (Steps 5 and 6) means the leftover energy has somewhere good to go.

**Om mani padme hum** compresses the whole proof into six syllables: the jewel (mani, compassion, Step 6) in the lotus (padme, wisdom that sees emptiness, Steps 2 and 3). Compassion held inside wisdom. Love, because it is all you.

## The proof in one breath

Everything caused must change; everything that changes has no fixed edge; nothing with no fixed edge is separate from me; what is not separate from me, I can only sanely love; and the loving is itself a cause, so the cycle ends where the love begins.
